Purpose and Audience

This framework is for professionals seeking to make more rigorous, resilient, and transparent decisions especially in complex environments. Inspired by military planning disciplines, it guides teams to challenge assumptions, broaden perspectives, and anticipate failure before it happens.

How to Use This Document

  • Move step-by-step through each question. Do not skip ahead.
  • Use the sub-questions and prompts to explore thinking from multiple angles.
  • Document all assumptions‚Äîespecially the ones that feel 'safe'.
  • Assign a 'devil's advocate' to every major idea or proposed solution.
  • Revisit answers often as conditions or insights evolve.

Key Concepts

  • Assumption: A belief or expectation we are treating as true without proof.
  • Red Team: A group or individual tasked with identifying flaws and blind spots in the current plan.
  • Wargaming: The act of testing an idea or solution against different future scenarios (both good and bad).
  • Pivot: A deliberate change in strategy based on new insights or external changes.
  • Pre mortem: A technique where the team imagines a future failure and works backward to understand what led there.

Question 1: What is happening in the market or environment and why?

Purpose: Gain an unfiltered, realistic understanding of external forces before deciding anything internally.

Steps to Follow

  1. List all known and assumed trends affecting your sector.
  2. Separate what you know from what you believe.
  3. Test those beliefs with fresh or external data.
  4. Map out optimistic, pessimistic, and neutral versions of the environment.

What to Question

  • What might we be missing because it's too obvious?
  • Where are we relying on stale or second-hand information?

Real Example (Retail Sector)

Situation: A retail chain believed online shopping was killing their stores.
Challenge Applied: They tested the assumption and found customers wanted stores but with different experiences.
Result: Pivoted to experiential retail, revenue up 20%.

Question 2: What have we been asked to achieve and why?

Purpose: Reframe the brief. Determine whether the stated objective is the root issue or just a visible symptom.

Steps to Follow

  1. Restate the ask in your own words.
  2. Ask: what would happen if we did nothing?
  3. Ask who benefits from this being solved‚Äîand who doesn't.
  4. Define at least two alternate problem statements.

What to Question

  • Is the problem urgent or just loud?
  • Have we solved this before‚Äîand failed?

Real Example (Tech Sector)

Situation: Tech company asked to "improve customer service response times"
Challenge Applied: Discovered the real issue was product complexity, not service speed
Result: Simplified product, reduced service calls by 60%

Question 3: What outcomes will define success?

Purpose: Separate activities from impact. Agree on how success will be measured in the real world.

Steps to Follow

  1. List desired outcomes as if you're reporting success two years from now.
  2. For each outcome, define what metric proves it happened.
  3. Define what failure would look like under the same metrics.

What to Question

  • Are our success metrics easy to game?
  • What trade-offs might come with 'winning' on paper?
Success Metric Unintended Consequence Balancing Metric
Faster delivery times Rushed work, errors increase Quality scores
Higher sales numbers Discounting erodes margins Profitability per sale
More features shipped Complexity overwhelms users User satisfaction score

Question 4: What options do we really have?

Purpose: Escape default thinking. Generate multiple, contrasting courses of action before choosing a path.

Steps to Follow

  1. List the obvious and safe option first.
  2. Force yourself to list at least three more radically different ones.
  3. Include a 'do nothing' scenario.
  4. Describe who would back each option‚Äîand who would resist.

What to Question

  • Why are we drawn to one option more than others?
  • What's the cost of not trying the boldest approach?

Option Generation Template

  • Option A: The expected solution (what everyone assumes we'll do)
  • Option B: The opposite approach (what if we did the reverse?)
  • Option C: The minimal version (least resources, fastest path)
  • Option D: The moonshot (unlimited resources, what then?)
  • Option E: Do nothing strategically (active choice to wait)

Question 5: How will each option perform under stress?

Purpose: Test every proposed path against adversity. Understand how each holds up when things go wrong.

Steps to Follow

  1. For each option, simulate best case, worst case, and unexpected disruption.
  2. Assign probabilities to outcomes where possible.
  3. Document what resilience looks like‚Äîand what breaks it.

What to Question

  • What assumptions fail under pressure?
  • What dependencies might not hold up?

Stress Test Scenarios

  • Budget cut by 50%
  • Key team members leave
  • Timeline compressed by half
  • Major competitor launches first
  • Technology doesn't work as expected
  • Market conditions change dramatically

Question 6: What could go wrong‚Äîand when will we know?

Purpose: Build early detection into your plan. Avoid surprise failure by planning for visible weak signals.

Steps to Follow

  1. Imagine your idea has failed. List five causes.
  2. For each cause, define what signs would show up 90 days early.
  3. Establish what actions would follow each warning sign.

What to Question

  • Do we have any 'kill criteria'‚Äîwhat failure triggers a stop or rethink?
  • What do we fear talking about‚Äîand why?
Potential Failure Early Warning Sign Trigger Action
Customer adoption too slow Week 4: Less than 10% target signups Emergency customer research
Technical debt mounting Sprint 3: Velocity drops 30% Pause features, fix foundation
Team burnout risk Month 2: Overtime exceeds 20% Add resources or cut scope

Question 7: How do we stay in control‚Äîwhile staying flexible?

Purpose: Ensure governance doesn't block responsiveness. Establish rules and roles that empower adaptability.

Steps to Follow

  1. Define who makes what decision‚Äîand how they're challenged.
  2. Define what can flex and what is fixed.
  3. Establish how feedback loops are collected and acted upon.

What to Question

  • What happens when bad news arrives‚Äîwho hears it and how fast?
  • Are there incentives to hide risk or avoid hard truths?

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Pitfall Description How to Catch It Early
Confirmation Bias Only looking for evidence that supports your preferred option Ask: "What would disprove this?" after every assumption
Group Think Avoiding conflict to maintain harmony Count dissenting views‚Äîif none, assign a devil's advocate
Anchoring Getting stuck on the first solution presented Generate 3 options before discussing any in detail
Planning Fallacy Underestimating time and resources needed Add 50% to estimates, then ask "What could double this?"
Sunk Cost Fallacy Continuing because you've already invested Review decisions as if starting from zero each month
